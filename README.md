    <h1>Colombia 2025 Liability Management Program<br />Tenders, TRS, and Euro Issuance</h1>
    <p class="subtitle">A practitioner’s overview for corporate actions and asset servicing professionals</p>

      This report summarizes the Republic of Colombia’s 2025 liability-management strategy, including multiple tender offers, a multi-bank Total Return Swap (TRS) structure, and significant Eurobond issuance. The focus is on operational flows, custody chains, and the broader sovereign balance-sheet story.
    </p>

    <!-- 1. Overview -->
    <h2 id="overview">1. Overview and Context</h2>
    <p>
      In 2025, the Republic of Colombia undertook a multi-stage liability-management program that combined:
    </p>
    <ul>
      <li>Traditional sovereign-led tender offers for outstanding U.S. Dollar and Euro-denominated global bonds,</li>
      <li>A large <strong>Total Return Swap (TRS)</strong> transaction with six global banks covering a basket of Colombia’s own bonds and other assets, and</li>
      <li>Record-size Eurobond issuance to refinance near-term obligations and diversify funding sources.</li>
    </ul>

    <p>
      The official announcements for Colombia’s November 2025 tender, including the financing condition and series-by-series treatment of “Old Bonds,” are in the
      <a href="https://www.prnewswire.com/news-releases/republic-of-colombia---announcement-of-offer-to-purchase-old-bonds-302616199.html" target="_blank">
        November 14, 2025 Offer to Purchase press release
      </a>
      and the
      <a href="https://www.prnewswire.com/news-releases/republic-of-colombia-announces-the-expiration-of-the-tender-offer-for-its-us-dollar-bonds-302621238.html" target="_blank">
        November 19, 2025 expiration announcement
      </a>.
    </p>

    <p>
      The broader financing strategy, including the TRS of approximately USD 9.4&nbsp;billion notional with six banks, has been analyzed in
      <a href="https://www.scotiabank.com/ca/en/about/economics/economics-publications/post.other-publications.latam-insights.latam-insights--october-10--2025-.html" target="_blank">
        Scotiabank’s Latam Insights (October 10, 2025)
      </a>
      and related Latam Daily and Latam Weekly pieces.
    </p>

    <div class="callout">
      <strong>Key idea:</strong> What looks like a “plain vanilla” tender from the perspective of SWIFT instructions and DTC/ICSD processing is, in reality, part of a multi-layered sovereign strategy that blends cash tenders, derivatives, and cross-currency issuance.
    </div>

          </a>,

            COP 43.4&nbsp;trillion domestic debt swap
          </a>,

    <!-- 3. Strategy -->
    <h2 id="strategy">3. Strategy: What Colombia Is Trying to Achieve</h2>

    <p>
      Viewed as a whole, Colombia’s 2025 program is not a single transaction but a <strong>three-pillar strategy</strong>:
    </p>

    <ol>
      <li>
        <strong>Reduce external hard-currency debt at a discount</strong><br />
        Through multiple tenders and the TRS, Colombia captures the discount between secondary-market prices and par value on long-dated global bonds.
      </li>
      <li>
        <strong>Diversify funding and investor base</strong><br />
        By reopening the Euro market and issuing €4.1&nbsp;billion in 2025, Colombia reduces exclusive dependence on the U.S. Dollar investor base and taps European institutional demand.
      </li>
      <li>
        <strong>Smooth the maturity profile and preserve cash</strong><br />
        Near-maturity bonds (like the Euro 2026s) are taken out and replaced with longer-dated paper. The TRS spreads the effective “buyback cost” over time instead of forcing a one-off cash drain.
      </li>
    </ol>

    <p>
      Scotiabank’s October 10, 2025 Latam Insights note frames these moves within a broader financing approach: buybacks of U.S. Dollar bonds, preference for Euro issuance, the TRS with six global banks, and limited reliance on multilaterals.
    </p>

    <!-- 4. TRS -->
    <h2 id="trs">4. The TRS Structure with Six Global Banks</h2>

    <p>
      The TRS is the most structurally novel part of Colombia’s 2025 toolkit. While the legal details sit in ISDA documentation rather than in the tender circular, the economics have been described in Scotiabank’s research and market commentary.
    </p>

    <div class="flex-columns">
      <div class="column">
        <div class="card">
          <h4>Reference Basket (Illustrative)</h4>
          <ul>
            <li>Approximately USD 5.4&nbsp;billion of Colombia U.S. Dollar global bonds (various maturities)</li>
            <li>COLTES (peso-denominated local Treasury bonds)</li>
            <li>Short-term local peso notes</li>
            <li>Approximately USD 1.5&nbsp;billion in U.S. Treasuries (hedging component)</li>
          </ul>
          <p style="font-size:11px; color:#555; margin-top:4px;">
            Figures and composition are approximate and based on Scotiabank Latam Weekly (September 12, 2025) and Latam Insights (October 10, 2025).
          </p>
        </div>
      </div>
      <div class="column">
        <div class="card">
          <h4>Flow of Economics</h4>
          <ul>
            <li><strong>Banks</strong> hold the assets on balance sheet and pay Colombia the total return (coupons plus price moves) on the reference basket.</li>
            <li><strong>Colombia</strong> pays a funding leg, reportedly indexed to low-rate benchmarks (including Swiss franc rates) to minimize all-in cost.</li>
            <li>At TRS maturity, Colombia is expected to take delivery of the bonds and retire them, converting synthetic exposure into legal ownership and final cancellation.</li>
          </ul>
        </div>
      </div>
    </div>

    <div class="callout">
      <strong>From a corporate actions perspective:</strong><br />
      On settlement date, custody systems simply show six banks accumulating global bonds. The TRS sits “behind the scenes” in derivatives books, but it is central to understanding why the banks were willing to act as tender offerors and warehouse sovereign risk.
    </div>

    <h2 id="ops">6. Operational and Corporate Actions Lens</h2>

    <p>
      For corporate actions and custody professionals, Colombia’s 2025 program is a case study in how “simple” event types can mask complex sovereign strategy.
    </p>

    <h3>6.1 What your teams actually see</h3>
    <ul>
      <li>Multiple voluntary tender events across several ISINs and currencies, disseminated via standard ICSD and DTC channels.</li>
      <li>Offer documentation circulated through the usual agents and posting sites; election capture and instruction deadlines at tight intervals.</li>
      <li>Settlement flows: investors → dealer-managers → purchasing banks → sovereign, with a Billing &amp; Delivery bank acting as settlement hub in at least some structures.</li>
      <li>Series-by-series pricing, cap management, and proration rules that must be translated accurately into entitlement calculations.</li>
    </ul>

    <h3>6.2 What sits behind the screen</h3>
    <ul>
      <li>Six global banks acting as <strong>principal offerors</strong> in some tenders, not just dealer-managers, because they are hedging a TRS exposure to Colombia.</li>
      <li>ISDA-governed derivative documentation that effectively pre-allocates the economic benefit of purchased bonds to the sovereign.</li>
      <li>Research providers decomposing the effects on yield curves, credit spreads, and future funding costs.</li>
      <li>Legal and regulatory engineering (for example, avoiding direct issuer-tender classification in some configurations) that influences how offers are framed and timed.</li>
    </ul>

    <p>
      For operations groups, the key lesson is that large sovereign liability-management operations may:
    </p>
    <ul>
      <li>Arrive as familiar event types (tenders, exchanges, switches) but carry unusual settlement flows (multiple principal banks; TRS-driven allocations).</li>
      <li>Require close coordination with product and legal teams, because seemingly minor terms (such as settlement date, maximum purchase amounts, acceptance priority) may be tightly tied to derivative hedges and cross-currency funding.</li>
      <li>Generate follow-up events (second or third tenders in the same calendar year) as the sovereign “stacks” several liability-management layers.</li>
    </ul>
