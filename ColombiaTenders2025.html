Colombia 2025 Liability Management Program
Tenders, TRS, and Euro Issuance

A practitioner’s overview for corporate actions and asset servicing professionals

This report summarizes the Republic of Colombia’s 2025 liability-management strategy, including multiple tender offers, a multi-bank Total Return Swap (TRS) structure, and significant Eurobond issuance. The focus is on operational flows, custody chains, and the broader sovereign balance-sheet story.

1. Overview and Context

In 2025, the Republic of Colombia undertook a multi-stage liability-management program that combined:

  • Traditional sovereign-led tender offers for outstanding U.S. Dollar and Euro-denominated global bonds,
  • A large Total Return Swap (TRS) transaction with six global banks covering a basket of Colombia’s own bonds and other assets, and
  • Record-size Eurobond issuance to refinance near-term obligations and diversify funding sources.

The official announcements for Colombia’s November 2025 tender, including the financing condition and series-by-series treatment of “Old Bonds,” are in the November 14, 2025 Offer to Purchase press release and the November 19, 2025 expiration announcement .

The broader financing strategy, including the TRS of approximately USD 9.4 billion notional with six banks, has been analyzed in Scotiabank’s Latam Insights (October 10, 2025) and related Latam Daily and Latam Weekly pieces.

Key idea: What looks like a “plain vanilla” tender from the perspective of SWIFT instructions and DTC/ICSD processing is, in reality, part of a multi-layered sovereign strategy that blends cash tenders, derivatives, and cross-currency issuance.

2. 2025 Transaction Timeline

  • May 2025 (Context)
    Record U.S. Dollar Global Issue and Tender Component
    Arnold & Porter reports advising Colombia on a US$3.8 billion dual-tranche global bond issue with an associated tender for 4.50% 2026 bonds. This foreshadows the liability-management approach that becomes more ambitious later in 2025.
  • August 2025
    Issuer-Led Tender for Long-Dated U.S. Dollar Globals
    Colombia launches a sovereign-led cash tender for several long-dated U.S. Dollar global bonds (2030–2061 maturities). The aim is to retire expensive long-end debt at a discount and demonstrate a proactive stance on the debt profile.
  • Early September 2025
    Largest-Ever LatAm Eurobond Issue and Euro 2026 Switch Tender
    Colombia reopens the Euro market with a €4.1 billion, three-tranche transaction ( Arnold & Porter, October 3, 2025 ): €1.45 billion due 2028, €1.45 billion due 2032, and €1.2 billion due 2036.
    In parallel, Colombia runs a tender for its €3.875% 2026 global bonds, prioritizing holders who reinvest into the new Euro issues. About €715 million of the 2026 bonds are taken out.
  • First week of September 2025
    Six-Bank TRS Hedge and U.S. Dollar Bond Purchases
    According to Scotiabank’s Latam Weekly (September 12, 2025) , six international banks purchase roughly USD 5.4 billion of Colombia global bonds in the first week of September to hedge a Total Return Swap structure with Colombia. Latam Insights (October 10) places the TRS notional at about USD 9.4 billion when including other assets such as COLTES, short-term local notes, and U.S. Treasuries.
  • October 9, 2025
    Largest Domestic Debt Swap in History
    In parallel, Colombia executes a COP 43.4 trillion domestic debt swap , further shaping its local-currency profile. This complements, rather than duplicates, the TRS and external liability-management operations.
  • November 14, 2025
    New Broad Tender for “Old Bonds” (U.S. Dollar and Euro)
    Colombia announces a new cash tender for a wide basket of “Old Bonds,” stating that the maximum purchase amount will be set at its sole discretion and subject to a financing condition, including new debt issuance. See the PR Newswire announcement of the offer .
  • November 19–21, 2025
    Tender Expiration for U.S. Dollar and Euro Bonds
    The U.S. Dollar bond component of the tender expires at 5:00 p.m. New York time on November 19, 2025, with Colombia indicating an expected aggregate purchase price of USD 4–6 billion for U.S. Dollar bonds alone. Non-U.S. Dollar bonds, including the Euro 2026s, are open for tender until November 21, 2025. See the November 19 expiration press release .

3. Strategy: What Colombia Is Trying to Achieve

Viewed as a whole, Colombia’s 2025 program is not a single transaction but a three-pillar strategy:

  1. Reduce external hard-currency debt at a discount
    Through multiple tenders and the TRS, Colombia captures the discount between secondary-market prices and par value on long-dated global bonds.
  2. Diversify funding and investor base
    By reopening the Euro market and issuing €4.1 billion in 2025, Colombia reduces exclusive dependence on the U.S. Dollar investor base and taps European institutional demand.
  3. Smooth the maturity profile and preserve cash
    Near-maturity bonds (like the Euro 2026s) are taken out and replaced with longer-dated paper. The TRS spreads the effective “buyback cost” over time instead of forcing a one-off cash drain.

Scotiabank’s October 10, 2025 Latam Insights note frames these moves within a broader financing approach: buybacks of U.S. Dollar bonds, preference for Euro issuance, the TRS with six global banks, and limited reliance on multilaterals.

4. The TRS Structure with Six Global Banks

The TRS is the most structurally novel part of Colombia’s 2025 toolkit. While the legal details sit in ISDA documentation rather than in the tender circular, the economics have been described in Scotiabank’s research and market commentary.

Reference Basket (Illustrative)

  • Approximately USD 5.4 billion of Colombia U.S. Dollar global bonds (various maturities)
  • COLTES (peso-denominated local Treasury bonds)
  • Short-term local peso notes
  • Approximately USD 1.5 billion in U.S. Treasuries (hedging component)

Figures and composition are approximate and based on Scotiabank Latam Weekly (September 12, 2025) and Latam Insights (October 10, 2025).

Flow of Economics

  • Banks hold the assets on balance sheet and pay Colombia the total return (coupons plus price moves) on the reference basket.
  • Colombia pays a funding leg, reportedly indexed to low-rate benchmarks (including Swiss franc rates) to minimize all-in cost.
  • At TRS maturity, Colombia is expected to take delivery of the bonds and retire them, converting synthetic exposure into legal ownership and final cancellation.
From a corporate actions perspective:
On settlement date, custody systems simply show six banks accumulating global bonds. The TRS sits “behind the scenes” in derivatives books, but it is central to understanding why the banks were willing to act as tender offerors and warehouse sovereign risk.

5.2 Maturity Profile (Conceptual)

Colombia used the 2025 operations to reduce near-term peaks and smooth the long end of the curve.

Bucket Before 2025 Program (Qualitative) After 2025 Program (Intended)
Short term (up to 2026) Significant hard-currency redemptions including Euro 2026s and various shorter U.S. Dollar lines. Euro 2026 exposure partially retired; some U.S. Dollar lines reduced; more refinancing into Euro and longer-dated paper.
Medium term (2027–2034) Growing cluster of U.S. Dollar bonds with chunky coupons; local curve relatively steep. New Euro bonds in 2028 and 2032, some U.S. Dollar lines reduced or synthetically managed via TRS; more balanced mix across currencies.

6. Operational and Corporate Actions Lens

For corporate actions and custody professionals, Colombia’s 2025 program is a case study in how “simple” event types can mask complex sovereign strategy.

6.1 What your teams actually see

  • Multiple voluntary tender events across several ISINs and currencies, disseminated via standard ICSD and DTC channels.
  • Offer documentation circulated through the usual agents and posting sites; election capture and instruction deadlines at tight intervals.
  • Settlement flows: investors → dealer-managers → purchasing banks → sovereign, with a Billing & Delivery bank acting as settlement hub in at least some structures.
  • Series-by-series pricing, cap management, and proration rules that must be translated accurately into entitlement calculations.

6.2 What sits behind the screen

  • Six global banks acting as principal offerors in some tenders, not just dealer-managers, because they are hedging a TRS exposure to Colombia.
  • ISDA-governed derivative documentation that effectively pre-allocates the economic benefit of purchased bonds to the sovereign.
  • Research providers decomposing the effects on yield curves, credit spreads, and future funding costs.
  • Legal and regulatory engineering (for example, avoiding direct issuer-tender classification in some configurations) that influences how offers are framed and timed.

For operations groups, the key lesson is that large sovereign liability-management operations may:

  • Arrive as familiar event types (tenders, exchanges, switches) but carry unusual settlement flows (multiple principal banks; TRS-driven allocations).
  • Require close coordination with product and legal teams, because seemingly minor terms (such as settlement date, maximum purchase amounts, acceptance priority) may be tightly tied to derivative hedges and cross-currency funding.
  • Generate follow-up events (second or third tenders in the same calendar year) as the sovereign “stacks” several liability-management layers.
